Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD) is a mental health condition where a person experiences repeated, unwanted thoughts (called obsessions) and feels driven to do certain actions or rituals (called compulsions) to reduce anxiety or prevent something bad from happening.

Important: People with OCD usually don’t want these thoughts or rituals — they happen because the brain is stuck in a “danger alarm” loop.

Obsessions (unwanted thoughts) can look like:

  • Fear of germs, contamination, or getting sick
  • Worry that something terrible will happen unless things are “just right”
  • Fear of making a mistake that harms someone
  • Uncomfortable thoughts that repeat even when you try to ignore them

Compulsions (rituals/actions) can look like:

  • Washing hands or cleaning over and over
  • Checking locks, doors, appliances, or homework repeatedly
  • Counting, tapping, repeating words, or arranging items until it feels “right”
  • Constantly seeking reassurance (“Are you sure?” “Did I mess up?”)

How OCD affects daily life:

  • Rituals can take a lot of time and make it hard to focus on school, work, or relationships
  • A person may avoid places, objects, or situations that trigger obsessions
  • It can feel exhausting, frustrating, or embarrassing — but it’s treatable

Common treatment options:

  • Therapy (ERP): Exposure and Response Prevention helps a person face triggers safely and resist compulsions
  • CBT: Cognitive Behavioral Therapy helps challenge scary thoughts and build healthier patterns
  • Medication: Some antidepressants (like SSRIs) can reduce symptoms for many people
  • Support: Education, family support, and routines can make treatment easier

If you think you might have OCD symptoms, talk to a trusted adult, counselor, doctor, or therapist. Getting help early can make a big difference.
