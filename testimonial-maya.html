Stan T.

Finding Light In The Darkness

"Being here helped me rediscover hope."

When I first started thinking about this series, I knew I wanted the opening story to be one I understood deeply, something that showed how hope can grow even in the darkest situations. I wanted a story about finding light again. Eventually, I realized the best story to begin with was my own.

I've struggled with depression for most of my life. Now in my early 40s, and having worked at MHAEM for almost three years, I finally feel like I'm doing work that matters. After spending most of my adult life in IT, miserable and unfulfilled, this job has given me direction and purpose. I used to roll my eyes when people said, "If you love what you do, you'll never work a day in your life," but now I truly understand what they meant. My belief that people are capable of goodness, and that helping each other is what makes us human, has always guided me.

But about a year and a half ago, something happened that made those beliefs feel like they were falling apart. I went into a deep depressive spiral, what I call my "valley days." Everything felt heavy, dull, and hopeless. I dragged myself to work feeling angry, bitter, and disconnected from the world around me.

Then, in the middle of one of those dark days, I remembered where I worked. Part of my job is answering calls and giving referrals. One day, a woman called crying, not because she was upset, but because she was grateful. Our PATH team had found her a warm bed after she'd been living out of her car. Her gratitude over something so basic reminded me how life-changing small acts of kindness can be.

More stories kept coming. I heard about colleagues who intervened in a suicidal crisis and got someone the help they desperately needed. I heard people describe our teams as "life savers." I saw how our programs helped clients find stability. It felt like the universe was sending me reminder after reminder of the quiet, powerful good that happens here every day.

These acts of compassion, big and small, began to pull me out of my darkness. My coworkers and mentors didn't just help others; without even knowing it, they were helping me too. Their kindness slowly rebuilt me, piece by piece, and reignited the sense of purpose I had lost. I'm still healing, but I'm no longer drowning in bitterness or anger. Climbing out of a valley takes time, but I'm moving forward day by day, guided by the hope this organization creates. And really, that's the heart of it: being here helped me rediscover hope.
